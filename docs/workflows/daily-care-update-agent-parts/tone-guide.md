# Daily care update tone guide

Purpose: define the voice, wording rules, examples, and review boundaries for daily customer update drafts. This is a drafting/review artifact only. It does not authorize autonomous customer sends, provider writes, photo publication, medical/behavior determinations, care-completion changes, or policy exceptions.

Status: draft guide derived from `docs/workflows/daily-care-update-agent-parts/inputs.md` and the customer-messaging draft/review policy. Daily/Pawgress or routine care updates remain draft-only by default until an approved deterministic send policy, template set, consent/media policy, and review path exist.

## Core voice

Daily updates should sound like a calm, observant care team member giving the owner a useful snapshot of the day.

Use a voice that is:

- warm: kind, pet-parent friendly, and reassuring without forced cheer;
- honest: every factual statement is source-backed and review-eligible;
- concise: short enough for staff to review quickly and for owners to understand at a glance;
- transparent: do not hide missing evidence, photo issues, care exceptions, or review needs;
- non-definitive on medical/behavior topics: describe approved observations, not diagnoses or predictions;
- not overly cutesy: avoid baby talk, excessive emojis, elaborate pet personas, or invented feelings.

Default structure for a routine customer draft:

1. Greeting using the owner or pet name only when available and approved for customer copy.
2. One to three source-backed care moments from the day.
3. Optional approved photo/media mention when a sendable media ref exists.
4. Brief close that does not promise outcomes or timing beyond approved policy.

Example routine draft:

> Hi Jordan — Milo had a nice day with us. He spent time outside, enjoyed his afternoon rest, and ate his dinner as noted by the care team. We’ll keep following his care plan and will share any updates that need your attention.

## Voice principles

### 1. Say what the care team observed, not what the model infers

Do:

- "Milo ate most of breakfast."
- "Luna spent time in individual play this afternoon."
- "Daisy rested quietly after her walk."
- "The team noted that Max was a little slower to settle today, so they kept his update in review."

Don't:

- "Milo loved breakfast."
- "Luna made tons of new friends."
- "Daisy was completely stress-free."
- "Max was anxious because he misses home."

Rule: use observed behaviors and approved task evidence. Do not narrate inner feelings, causes, or certainty unless the source is an approved customer-safe note and the wording remains non-diagnostic.

### 2. Keep positive updates specific but modest

Normal/positive moments should feel pleasant without exaggeration. Prefer one concrete detail over broad praise.

Good patterns:

- "[Pet] enjoyed some quiet rest after playtime."
- "[Pet] joined their scheduled enrichment time and was engaged with the activity."
- "[Pet] ate [all/most/some] of [meal] according to today’s care note."
- "[Pet] had a relaxed afternoon and settled well for rest time."
- "We have a photo from today attached for review/send once approved."

Avoid overstatement:

- "Best day ever!"
- "[Pet] was perfect all day."
- "[Pet] absolutely loved every second."
- "[Pet] made friends with everyone."
- "We know [pet] is having the time of their life."

Rewrite examples:

| Overstated | Better |
| --- | --- |
| "Bella had the best day ever!" | "Bella had a good day with us and enjoyed her afternoon playtime." |
| "Cooper made friends with every dog." | "Cooper participated in his approved play session today." |
| "Luna loved dinner." | "Luna ate her dinner as noted by the care team." |
| "Max was happy all day." | "Max was bright during his afternoon check-in and rested quietly afterward." |

### 3. Make neutral details calm and actionable

Neutral or mildly imperfect details should be factual, short, and proportionate. The goal is to inform owners without making ordinary variation sound like an emergency.

Good patterns:

- "[Pet] ate part of breakfast and was offered dinner as scheduled. The team will keep noting meals."
- "[Pet] took a little extra time to settle this morning, then rested quietly after lunch."
- "The team did not get a sendable photo yet, so this update is text-only for review."
- "[Pet] was quieter than usual during the afternoon note, so the team is keeping the update in review."

Avoid alarm or unsupported reassurance:

- "[Pet] refused food and might be sick."
- "Don’t worry, [pet] is totally fine."
- "[Pet] was sad today."
- "We couldn’t get a photo, but everything is perfect."

Rewrite examples:

| Mildly imperfect source fact | Customer-safe draft candidate | Internal/review note when needed |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Ate some breakfast, no other concern noted | "Milo ate some of breakfast today, and the team continued his normal care routine." | None if meal wording is approved and non-sensitive. |
| Took extra time to settle | "Luna took a little extra time to settle this morning and rested quietly later in the day." | Review if this could indicate stress/anxiety or if behavior notes are unapproved. |
| No usable photo | "Today’s update is text-only because the available photo was not ready for customer use." | Create/recommend staff review or replacement-photo task when policy requires media. |
| Staff note says "possible limp" | Do not include in routine copy. | "Suppressed from customer copy: possible health/safety observation requires manager/medical review." |

### 4. Preserve transparency without turning updates into reports

A daily update should not be a full care log. Keep customer copy brief, but preserve factual transparency in the review packet.

Customer copy should include:

- a few approved, customer-safe facts;
- material routine care details the policy requires for daily updates;
- approved media mention only if media is sendable;
- neutral acknowledgement of review/text-only state when needed and approved.

Internal reviewer notes should carry:

- evidence refs and source timestamps;
- omitted/suppressed facts and why they were omitted;
- missing photo/media reason;
- required review gate;
- sensitivity flags;
- unsupported spans rejected by validators.

Do not solve brevity by deleting risk. If a concerning or required fact exists but is not approved for customer copy, omit it from the customer message and record it as a review/suppression reason.

## Wording rules by content type

### Meals and feeding

Use meal wording only from reviewed care task evidence or approved staff notes.

Do:

- "Milo ate breakfast as noted by the care team."
- "Bella ate most of dinner today."
- "Cooper was offered lunch according to his care plan."

Use review language when:

- the meal was refused or missed;
- the source is raw/free-text only;
- the note conflicts with the feeding instruction;
- the wording could imply illness, negligence, or medication interaction.

Review language:

- "Feeding note needs staff review before customer wording: source says `[brief redacted reason]`."
- "Do not draft routine meal copy until the feeding exception is reviewed."

Avoid:

- "Milo refused food because he is upset."
- "Our staff forgot breakfast."
- "Bella is eating normally now" unless approved evidence defines and supports that claim.

### Medication, allergies, and medical/health observations

Daily update drafts must not diagnose, interpret symptoms, advise treatment, or state medical conclusions. Medication facts require reviewed medication instructions and authorized staff evidence.

Do, only when approved for customer copy:

- "The team followed the medication instructions on file today."
- "A care note involving medication is ready for staff review."

Prefer internal/review language for most medication or health details:

- "Manager/medical review required before mentioning medication administration in customer copy."
- "Suppressed from routine update: staff note includes a health-related observation that needs review."

Avoid:

- "Max seems sick."
- "Daisy probably has an upset stomach."
- "Give Bella a different dose tonight."
- "The limp is nothing to worry about."
- "His allergies are under control."

### Mood, temperament, play, and behavior

Use routine, approved observations. Do not label temperament, assign motives, promise group-play eligibility, or blame pets.

Do:

- "Luna was calm during her afternoon rest period."
- "Cooper participated in his scheduled individual play time."
- "Milo enjoyed a quieter enrichment session today."

Use review language when:

- there is aggression, bite, group-play restriction, behavior review, anxiety/stress flag, incident, safety concern, or conflicting note;
- the draft would affect future eligibility or playgroup status.

Review language:

- "BehaviorReview required before customer-facing wording about today’s playgroup note."
- "Suppress routine upbeat copy until manager/behavior review resolves the safety note."

Avoid:

- "Luna was aggressive."
- "Cooper failed group play."
- "Milo was anxious because he missed you."
- "Bella is safe for group play now."
- "Your dog caused a problem."

### Bathroom/elimination

Bathroom status is currently a provisional care-note/task-evidence field. Use only if the source is approved and the policy says it belongs in daily updates.

Do:

- "Milo had his scheduled potty breaks today."
- "The team noted a normal bathroom break this afternoon." only if `normal` is an approved customer-safe value.

Use review language when:

- the note is abnormal, missing, ambiguous, or medically suggestive;
- the wording could imply diagnosis or treatment.

Avoid:

- "Milo has a stomach issue."
- "Bella’s bathroom habits are fixed now."
- detailed elimination descriptions unless approved and necessary.

### Photos and media

Only mention a photo when a reviewed, sendable media ref exists or when approved policy allows a transparent text-only explanation. Never imply a photo exists if it is unavailable, poor quality, wrong pet, privacy-sensitive, or not approved for customer use.

Do:

- "We have a photo from today ready for staff review."
- "Today’s update is text-only because the available photo was not ready for customer use."
- "A replacement-photo task is recommended before sending this update." for internal output.

Use review language when:

- another pet, customer, staff member, name tag, incident context, unsafe/unflattering scene, wrong pet, or facility-sensitive area appears;
- consent/media policy is missing;
- photo is required but unavailable.

Avoid:

- "Photo attached!" when no approved media ref exists.
- "We couldn’t get a good picture, but trust us, everything is great."
- "Here’s a webcam snapshot" unless webcam/customer-use policy approves it.

### Incidents, safety, complaints, and unresolved concerns

Incident, safety, illness/injury, medication exception, behavior/aggression, customer complaint, payment/legal/privacy, or unresolved-care signals should suppress routine upbeat updates until the appropriate review gate supplies approved wording.

Do not blend a serious concern into a cheerful routine update.

Internal/review language:

- "Suppress routine daily update: incident/safety signal present; ManagerApproval required before customer-facing wording."
- "Customer copy withheld pending approved incident disposition."
- "Draft only after manager-approved language is provided for the incident follow-up."

Avoid:

- "Aside from a small incident, Max had a great day!"
- "Another dog bit Milo but he is fine."
- "Our staff handled it wrong, but we fixed it."
- "There is nothing to worry about" unless approved incident/medical/safety wording explicitly supports it.

## Do / don't examples

| Goal | Do | Don't |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Warm opening | "Hi Jordan — Milo had a good day with us." | "OMG Milo had the BEST day!!!" |
| Source-backed meal note | "Milo ate most of breakfast today." | "Milo loved breakfast." |
| Positive play note | "Bella enjoyed her scheduled playtime this afternoon." | "Bella made friends with everyone." |
| Neutral mood note | "Cooper took a little extra time to settle, then rested quietly." | "Cooper was sad and missed you." |
| Text-only media transparency | "Today’s update is text-only because the available photo was not ready for customer use." | "No photo today, but everything was perfect." |
| Missing evidence | "This update needs staff review because the meal note is incomplete." | "Milo had a normal meal" when the meal note is missing. |
| Health boundary | "A health-related note needs manager/medical review before customer wording." | "Daisy probably has an upset stomach." |
| Incident boundary | "Routine update suppressed pending manager review." | "There was a little incident, but it’s all fine." |
| Blame boundary | "We are reviewing the details and will follow up after manager review." | "The staff forgot" / "Your dog caused it." |
| Promise boundary | "We’ll keep following the care plan on file." | "We guarantee he’ll be completely settled tomorrow." |

## Banned / avoid phrasing

Treat these as validator examples. Some may become allowed only if a future approved template or manager-provided exact wording explicitly authorizes them.

### Never use in auto-send or routine draft copy

- "best day ever"
- "perfect all day"
- "completely fine"
- "nothing to worry about"
- "guaranteed"
- "we promise"
- "definitely"
- "for sure"
- "cured"
- "diagnosed"
- "sick" as a conclusion from observation
- "anxious" / "depressed" / "aggressive" as unsupported labels
- "failed group play"
- "your dog caused"
- "our staff forgot"
- "staff mistake"
- "we are liable" / "we are not liable"
- "refund approved" or timing/amount language without payment/manager approval
- "photo attached" without approved media ref
- "webcam snapshot" without approved camera/customer-use policy
- "normal" for meals, bathroom, health, or behavior unless `normal` is an approved source value

### Avoid unless exact approved wording is supplied

- legal/liability terms;
- medical, medication, allergy, vaccine, treatment, or eligibility conclusions;
- behavior/aggression/group-play eligibility conclusions;
- incident details or fault descriptions;
- policy exception explanations;
- payment/refund/deposit/waiver language;
- promises about staff response time, future behavior, service outcomes, photos, or availability;
- emojis, pet baby talk, pet POV narration, or elaborate personality claims.

## Review language vs auto-send language

Daily updates are draft/review by default. Use customer-ready wording only when all required facts are source-backed, customer-safe, non-sensitive, and allowed by the policy context. Use review language instead of customer copy whenever the safest output is a reviewer decision, suppression reason, or staff task.

### Customer-ready draft language may be used when all are true

- Every factual statement is supported by approved evidence refs.
- Content is routine and low sensitivity.
- No incident, health, medication, allergy, behavior/aggression, safety, complaint, payment, legal/privacy, policy-exception, or unresolved-care signal is present.
- Photo/media mention has an approved sendable media ref, or the update is transparently text-only under approved policy.
- Recipient/channel/consent/quiet-hours/suppression state is compatible with the workflow.
- The output still sets `requires_approval = true` unless a future deterministic send policy explicitly authorizes this exact category/template/path.

Customer-ready draft example:

> Hi Sam — Bella had a good day with us. She joined her scheduled playtime, rested quietly afterward, and ate most of dinner. Today’s update is ready for staff review before sending.

### Review language is required when any are true

- source facts are missing, stale, conflicting, ambiguous, raw/free-text-only, or unapproved;
- a required care area has no evidence;
- a photo is required but unavailable, poor quality, wrong-pet, privacy-sensitive, or not approved;
- any health, medication, allergy, feeding exception, bathroom concern, behavior, incident, safety, complaint, payment, legal/privacy, or policy-exception signal appears;
- customer copy would require diagnosis, reassurance, blame assignment, staff-defense language, or a guarantee;
- the draft would mention another pet/customer/staff member without approved privacy policy;
- a validator cannot cite every customer-visible claim to approved facts.

Review-output examples:

- "Customer copy suppressed: staff note includes a possible health/safety observation. Manager/medical review required before wording."
- "Needs staff review: meal evidence is incomplete for today’s update; do not generate routine meal copy."
- "Needs photo review: available media may include another pet/customer context; do not attach or mention it in customer copy."
- "Needs manager review: behavior note may affect group-play eligibility; routine upbeat update withheld."
- "Rejected draft span: `nothing to worry about` is unsupported reassurance. Rewrite or route to review."

## Staff-defensive, blame, and trust language

Daily updates should not sound defensive, evasive, or like the team is arguing with the customer. They should also not admit fault or assign blame without approved manager/legal wording.

Use:

- "The team is reviewing the details."
- "We are checking the note and will follow up once it has been reviewed."
- "This update needs manager review before customer wording."
- "We found a mismatch in the care note and are resolving it internally before sending an update."

Avoid:

- "The staff did everything right."
- "This was not our fault."
- "The customer gave us wrong instructions."
- "The system messed up."
- "Your dog was the problem."
- "We forgot / missed / failed" unless manager/legal-approved wording explicitly uses those terms.

## Concision rules

Keep daily update copy short enough that the owner can quickly understand the day and staff can quickly approve it.

Guidelines:

- SMS/portal short update: one to three short sentences.
- Email/app longer update: greeting plus two to four short sentences, or a tiny bullet list if product policy supports it.
- One update should usually include at most three routine care facts.
- Do not list every task completed unless policy requires it.
- Do not explain internal workflow terms to the customer.
- Move source refs, review reasons, validation failures, and omitted facts into internal reviewer notes.

Concise rewrite examples:

| Too long | Better |
| --- | --- |
| "Milo started the morning with outside time, then came back in, then staff checked his room, then he had rest time, then he ate dinner, and then we checked on him again." | "Milo enjoyed outside time, rested comfortably, and ate dinner as noted by the care team." |
| "Because our system did not receive an image that our policy says can be sent to customers, this message cannot include a photo at this time." | "Today’s update is text-only because no approved customer-use photo is available yet." |
| "There is a possible issue with the staff note that may or may not mean something, so we need someone to look at it." | "Needs staff review: today’s care note is ambiguous and should not be used in customer copy yet." |

## Output expectations for daily-care update agents

A generated daily-care update packet should separate customer copy from review metadata.

Customer draft fields should contain only:

- customer-visible update text;
- optional subject/title when channel requires it;
- approved pet/customer names and routine facts;
- no raw source refs, internal note text, reviewer debate, or policy jargon.

Internal review fields should contain:

- `tone_status`: `customer_ready_draft`, `needs_staff_review`, `needs_manager_review`, `suppressed`, or `rejected_for_rewrite`;
- `tone_reason`: specific reason, such as unsupported reassurance, missing meal evidence, unapproved photo, health-related note, behavior review, incident signal, privacy risk, or overlong/channel mismatch;
- `facts_used`: exact source-backed facts used in customer copy;
- `omitted_or_suppressed_facts`: facts withheld and why;
- `banned_phrases_detected`: exact spans that must be removed;
- `review_gates`: e.g. `CustomerMessageApproval`, `ManagerApproval`, `MedicalDocumentReview`, `BehaviorReview`, `PrivacyReview`;
- `rewrite_needed`: true when tone or safety rules fail.

Default final rule: if the agent cannot produce a warm, honest, concise, source-backed update without diagnosis, guarantees, blame, unsupported reassurance, privacy risk, or hidden sensitive facts, it should not produce customer-ready copy. It should produce a review reason, suppression reason, or internal staff task recommendation instead.
